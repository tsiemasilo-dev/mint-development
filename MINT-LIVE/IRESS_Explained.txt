--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
0. THE ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY (READ THIS FIRST)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The original document is NOT software and it is NOT something you install. It is
a "shopping list" / contract specification that Mint wrote FOR a financial data
company called IRESS (their data service is branded "IRIS"). It lists every single
type of market data Mint wants IRESS to deliver, how fresh that data must be, and
how reliably it must arrive. The aim is to give Mint the same depth of data that
big professional investment banks get from Bloomberg — hence the phrase
"Bloomberg-grade" all over the document.

Think of it like a restaurant handing a supplier an extremely detailed order form:
"Here is every ingredient we need, how fresh it must be, and how fast you must
deliver it." Each line in the spec is one data "feed" Mint is asking IRESS to
switch on.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. KEY WORDS, IN PLAIN ENGLISH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- IRESS .............. The vendor (supplier). A real company that sells financial
                       market data, especially for the JSE (Johannesburg Stock
                       Exchange).
- IRIS .............. The name of IRESS's market-data product.
- OEMS .............. "Order & Execution Management System." A professional
                       trading cockpit: see prices -> decide -> place orders ->
                       track fills -> report. Mint wants to build one.
- OMS / PMS ......... Order Management / Portfolio Management System. Cousins of
                       the OEMS for managing orders and portfolios.
- API ............... The technical "doorway" one piece of software uses to ask
                       another for data. IRESS exposes data through an API.
- REST .............. A style of API where your app ASKS a question and gets one
                       answer back (good for "give me today's price").
- WebSocket (WS) .... A style of API where data STREAMS to you continuously
                       without re-asking (good for live, tick-by-tick prices).
- Endpoint .......... A single web address that returns one specific kind of data.
- Entitlement ....... A permission switch. You only receive the data types you
                       have paid/licensed for. No entitlement = no data.
- Sandbox vs Prod ... "Sandbox" is the safe practice environment with fake/sample
                       data. "Production" (prod) is the real, live environment.
- Tenant ............ Your dedicated account on IRESS's system
                       (e.g. "mint-sandbox", "mint-prod").
- OAuth 2.0 ......... The secure login method. Your app trades a client ID +
                       secret for a temporary "access token" used on every call.
- L1 / Level 1 ...... "Top of book" — the single best buy price and best sell
                       price for a stock, plus last traded price.
- L2 / Level 2 ...... "Market depth" — the full ladder of buy and sell orders at
                       many price levels. Much richer (and more expensive).
- SLA ............... "Service Level Agreement" — promised speed and uptime.
- FSCA .............. South Africa's financial regulator. Some data must be kept
                       as evidence for them.

2. WHAT THE DOCUMENT IS ASKING IRESS TO DELIVER (THE BIG PICTURE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The spec is divided into ~21 sections and roughly 150 individual data feeds.
Grouped simply, they cover:

  1.  Logging in & staying connected (authentication, health checks, rate limits)
  2.  The master list of all stocks/bonds ("reference data" / "security master")
  3.  Share prices: live quotes, order-book depth, and 25 years of history
  4.  Stock-market indices (JSE Top 40, S&P 500, etc.)
  5.  Bonds (government & company debt) and their analytics
  6.  Interest rates and yield curves (JIBAR, repo, prime, etc.)
  7.  Money-market instruments (T-Bills, NCDs, etc.)
  8.  Foreign exchange (USD/ZAR and other currency pairs)
  9.  Commodities & futures (gold, oil, maize, etc.)
  10. Macro-economics (inflation, GDP, interest-rate decisions)
  11. News (Reuters, Dow Jones, plus JSE company announcements = "SENS")
  12. Company fundamentals (financial statements, analyst ratings, ESG)
  13. Funds / ETFs (prices, holdings, performance)
  14. Derivatives & structured products (options, futures) — mostly "Phase 2"
  15. Risk & compliance data (sanctions lists, trading halts)
  16. Actually placing trades (order execution via the "FIX" protocol)
  17. The live streaming (WebSocket) rules
  18. Speed & uptime promises (SLAs)
  19. Security & governance
  20. The acceptance checklist (how the vendor proves it's done correctly)
  21. A map showing which feed powers which screen in the Mint app


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. SECTION-BY-SECTION, IN SIMPLE ENGLISH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 1 — AUTHENTICATION & INFRASTRUCTURE
  What it means: The plumbing for getting in and staying in.
  - Your app logs in with OAuth and receives an access token that lasts 1 hour;
    you refresh it at ~55 minutes so you're never locked out.
  - Every request must carry a header naming your tenant (mint-prod).
  - There's a way to ask "what am I allowed to see?" (entitlements).
  - There are health-check endpoints so your app can tell if the feed is up; if it
    goes down, your app's "circuit breaker" can switch to a backup or show a
    friendly message instead of crashing.
  - Rate limits: at least 600 requests/minute and 50 simultaneous live streams.
  - Audit logs must be exportable daily (kept as evidence for the regulator).

SECTION 2 — REFERENCE DATA (THE SECURITY MASTER)
  What it means: The foundational dictionary of every tradable thing.
  - The master list of all securities, refreshed nightly, with every possible ID
    code (ISIN, JSE code, Bloomberg ticker, etc.), the company name, sector,
    currency, exchange, listing date, lot size, and more.
  - Why it matters MOST: the document says "without it nothing else reconciles."
    Every price, news item, and trade links back to this list. If your app can't
    match IRESS's stock IDs to your own records, nothing else lines up. This is
    always step one of any integration.
  - Also includes exchange info, holiday calendars, sector classifications,
    issuer/credit-rating data, and CORPORATE ACTIONS (dividends, share splits,
    mergers, name changes) — crucial for keeping prices and holdings accurate.

SECTION 3 — EQUITIES (SHARE PRICES)
  What it means: Everything about individual share prices.
  - Level 1 (3.1): best bid/ask, last price, day high/low, volume, etc. — updated
    every second or live via streaming. This is the bread-and-butter price data.
  - Level 2 (3.2): the full order book (10 levels of buyers and sellers) plus a
    "time & sales" tape of every trade. Powerful but heavier/costlier. Mandatory
    for JSE here; "Phase 2" (later) for global markets.
  - History (3.3): up to 25 years of daily prices, intraday bars, raw ticks, and
    "total-return" series (price growth WITH dividends reinvested). Needed for
    charts and performance reporting.
  - Analytics (3.4): pre-calculated risk and performance numbers (volatility,
    beta, drawdown, Value-at-Risk, style factors) so Mint doesn't have to compute
    them itself.

SECTION 4 — INDICES
  What it means: The "market thermometers."
  - Live values and history for JSE indices (ALSI, Top 40, SWIX...) and global
    ones (S&P 500, Nasdaq, FTSE, VIX...), including their member companies and
    weights, and a sector "heatmap."

SECTION 5 — BONDS (FIXED INCOME)
  What it means: Government and company debt, plus the heavy maths.
  - The bond universe, live prices/yields, cash-flow schedules, credit ratings,
    auction results, and inflation-linked bond data. Includes professional
    analytics (yield-to-maturity, duration, convexity, spreads) that normally
    require a Bloomberg terminal.

SECTION 6 — YIELD CURVES & RATES
  What it means: The cost of money over time.
  - Official SA interest-rate benchmarks (JIBAR, SABOR, ZARONIA, SARB repo,
    prime), full yield curves for ZAR/USD/EUR/GBP, swaps, and central-bank policy
    rates with the market's expected future path.

SECTION 7 — MONEY MARKET
  What it means: Short-term cash instruments.
  - Treasury Bills, Negotiable Certificates of Deposit, commercial paper, repos,
    their rates, auctions, and a short-term curve.

SECTION 8 — FX (FOREIGN EXCHANGE)
  What it means: Currency exchange rates.
  - Live spot rates (USD/ZAR etc.), forward rates, official daily "fixings" used
    for valuations, 25 years of history, and a full cross-rate matrix.

SECTION 9 — COMMODITIES & FUTURES
  What it means: Physical goods and contracts on them.
  - Gold, platinum, Brent/WTI oil, natural gas, copper, iron ore, and SA
    agricultural products (maize, wheat, soya), plus futures contracts and option
    chains (with "Greeks" — sensitivity measures).

SECTION 10 — MACRO-ECONOMICS
  What it means: The health of the economy.
  - An economic calendar (when CPI/GDP/rate decisions are released), the actual
    data series, SARB and StatsSA feeds, global indicators, and analyst consensus
    forecasts.

SECTION 11 — NEWS
  What it means: What's happening, as it happens.
  - Live news from Reuters, Dow Jones, Moneyweb, Business Day, etc., tagged by
    company and sentiment; full-text search; and crucially SENS — the official
    JSE channel where listed companies publish announcements (results, cautionary
    notices, director dealings). Mint wants SENS within 5 seconds of publication.

SECTION 12 — FUNDAMENTALS & ESTIMATES (RESEARCH)
  What it means: Deep company analysis.
  - 10 years of financial statements, valuation ratios (P/E, ROE...), analyst
    forecasts and Buy/Hold/Sell recommendations, ownership data, ESG scores, and
    earnings-call transcripts.

SECTION 13 — ETFs, UNIT TRUSTS & FUNDS
  What it means: Pooled investment products.
  - Fund prices (NAV), holdings, performance over many periods, and risk metrics.

SECTION 14 — DERIVATIVES & STRUCTURED PRODUCTS (mostly Phase 2 / later)
  What it means: Advanced contracts.
  - Single-stock futures, index futures, currency futures, and implied-volatility
    surfaces. Marked as acceptable to deliver later.

SECTION 15 — RISK & COMPLIANCE DATASETS
  What it means: Safety and legal guardrails.
  - Live list of halted/suspended stocks, sanctions lists (OFAC, UN, EU, FSCA),
    Politically Exposed Persons, per-stock trading flags, and short-sale
    disclosures. Used to BLOCK trades that shouldn't happen (the "pre-trade
    rebalance gate").

SECTION 16 — ORDER EXECUTION
  What it means: Actually buying and selling.
  - A "FIX" gateway (the industry-standard messaging protocol brokers use) to
    place, cancel, and amend orders; a "drop-copy" feed mirroring all fills for
    risk; post-trade allocations; and best-execution evidence. Note: this comes
    from the wider IRESS Group, not the IRIS data product itself.

SECTION 17 — STREAMING (WEBSOCKET) RULES
  What it means: How the live firehose behaves.
  - You connect once and "subscribe" to channels (quotes, depth, news...). The
    feed must auto-reconnect with no missed messages, send a heartbeat every 15s,
    give a full snapshot on subscribe then live updates, and support large numbers
    of simultaneous symbols.

SECTION 18 — SLAs & OPERATIONAL REQUIREMENTS
  What it means: The speed and reliability promises.
  - Typical response under 80 milliseconds, live JSE updates under 100ms, 99.95%
    uptime during market hours, defined maintenance windows, fast incident
    response, and quick correction of bad data.

SECTION 19 — SECURITY & GOVERNANCE
  What it means: Keeping it safe and compliant.
  - Mandatory OAuth login, IP allow-listing, encryption in transit (TLS 1.3),
    signed entitlement files, annual security audit reports (SOC 2), and a
    right-to-audit clause.

SECTION 20 — ACCEPTANCE CHECKLIST (SIGN-OFF GATE)
  What it means: How Mint proves the vendor did it right before going live.
  - e.g. prices must match the JSE official close within one tick; 10-year
    total-return series must reconcile to official factsheets within 5 basis
    points; SENS announcements must arrive within 5 seconds; the live feed must
    survive a forced disconnection with zero data loss; both sandbox and prod must
    be live with separate credentials.

SECTION 21 — MAPPING TO THE MINT APP SCREENS
  What it means: Which feed powers which part of the app. Examples:
  - Dashboard tiles  -> indices + FX + quotes
  - Equities blotter -> live quotes + depth + trading halts
  - Yield curve/macro-> rates + macro calendar
  - News feed        -> news + SENS
  - Rebalance gate   -> halts + corporate actions + snapshots
  - Compliance       -> halts + sanctions lists
  - Client reporting -> total-return + performance + fund data

4. WHAT IS ACTUALLY REQUIRED FOR THIS TO WORK WITH THE MINT APP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Two sides have to come together.

A) WHAT IRESS (THE VENDOR) MUST GIVE YOU
   1. Two accounts (tenants): a sandbox (testing) and production (live).
   2. OAuth credentials — a client ID + secret (kept as protected secrets, never
      hard-coded). These are rotated every 90 days.
   3. Entitlements — the permission switches for each data type you license
      (JSE prices, news, bonds, etc.). You only receive what you pay for.
   4. The base web addresses:
        REST:      https://api.iress.com/v1
        WebSocket: wss://stream.iress.com/v1
   5. A signed "entitlement file" describing exactly what you're allowed to see.

B) WHAT THE MINT APP MUST BE BUILT TO DO
   1. AUTHENTICATE: fetch an access token via OAuth and auto-refresh it before it
      expires (~every 55 minutes). Tokens are kept server-side, never in the
      browser.
   2. CALL THE DATA: use REST for one-off requests and WebSocket for live streams.
   3. STORE THE SECURITY MASTER FIRST: import IRESS's reference list and map their
      stock identifiers to Mint's existing securities records so prices reconcile.
      This is non-negotiable step one.
   4. RESPECT THE RULES: handle rate limits (back off when told), watch the health
      endpoints, and fall back gracefully if the feed drops.
   5. STORE DATA CORRECTLY: keep price units consistent and convert where needed
      (Mint's existing data stores some prices in cents, so any new feed must be
      normalised to match before it's used in calculations).
   6. SECURE THE SECRETS: client ID/secret and tokens live only in protected
      server-side configuration.

5. HOW THIS COMPARES TO WHAT MINT HAS TODAY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Today, Mint gets market data the simple, low-cost way: a custom proxy that pulls
from Yahoo Finance, with prices stored in a database table. That is completely
fine for a consumer-facing investing app.

What this document describes is a MAJOR step up — a professional, institutional-
grade data subscription. Being honest about it:

  - It is a LARGE, EXPENSIVE, LONG-TERM integration, not a quick feature.
    Institutional JSE Level-2 data, bond analytics, and news wires like Reuters
    carry significant monthly licensing costs and formal contracts.
  - The VAST MAJORITY of what's listed is well beyond what the Mint app currently
    does or needs. Mint shows portfolios, strategies, and basic prices — it does
    not need bond convexity, option Greeks, or a FIX order gateway to function.
  - The sensible approach is PHASED: start with the few feeds Mint would actually
    use, prove them in the sandbox, then expand only as the product grows.

6. A REALISTIC "START SMALL" SHORTLIST
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
If Mint wanted to begin a first, minimal IRESS connection that matches how the app
works today, these are the feeds that would actually matter first:

  MUST-HAVE TO START
  - Section 1  Authentication (you can't do anything without logging in)
  - Section 2  Reference data / security master (map stocks to Mint's records)
  - Section 3.1 Level 1 equity quotes (live JSE prices — the core need)
  - Section 3.3 Historical prices + total return (powers charts & performance)

  NICE TO HAVE SOON AFTER
  - Section 4  Indices (dashboard context)
  - Section 11 News + SENS (company announcements)
  - Section 2.7 / 15.4 Corporate actions + trading-halt flags (keeps holdings and
    the rebalance logic correct and safe)

  LEAVE FOR LATER (only if the product expands into pro trading)
  - Level 2 depth, bonds, money market, FX, commodities, derivatives, fundamentals
    research, FIX order execution.

7. BOTTOM LINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
- This document is a VENDOR BRIEF: Mint telling IRESS exactly what data service to
  build and deliver. Nothing in it runs inside the Mint codebase yet.
- To make it work with the app you need: (1) IRESS to grant sandbox credentials +
  entitlements, then (2) a connector built into Mint that logs in, pulls/streams
  the specific feeds you choose, and maps them onto Mint's existing securities
  data — starting with the security master and live JSE prices.
- Treat the full document as the long-term "wish list," not the day-one build.
  Start with the must-have shortlist in Section 6 above.
